## Course Description

**Introduction to Astronomy** is a three-credit hour course designed for students with little to no prior experience in astronomy. This course is lecture-based but highly interactive, emphasizing active engagement through collaborative learning and hands-on experiences. Students will explore astronomical phenomena through the principles of physical science. Topics include:  

- The night sky and our solar system  
- Fundamental principles of astronomy  
- Nature of light and the electromagnetic spectrum  
- Stellar evolution  
- Galaxies, dark matter, and the expanding Universe

## Specific Learning Objectives (SLOs)
- Investigate the vastness of the Universe and understand its evolution from the Big Bang to the present.
- Apply Kepler's and Newton's laws to comprehend the orbital motion of celestial bodies.
- Compare and interpret the concepts of apparent and absolute magnitude in stars. 
- Determine stellar distances using parallax methods (both distance and spectroscopic).
- Analyze and explain the electromagnetic spectrum and its significance in astronomy.
- Investigate the nature of different types of telescopes and the effects of Earth's atmosphere on their operations.
- Analyze blackbody radiation and relate it to the temperature of celestial objects
- Analyze types of spectra to decipher the composition, temperature, motion, and physical properties of celestial objects.
- Investigate the Doppler shift in spectral lines and utilize it to analyze the motion of stars and galaxies.
- Utilize the H-R diagram for stellar classification and evolution.
- Identify eclipsing binary star systems and measurement of stellar masses and radii
- Detect exoplanets using doppler and transient methods.
- Explore the scale of the Milky Way Galaxy and classify the various types of galaxies
- Evaluate the significance of dark matter and analyze the rotational curve
- Investigate the expansion of the Universe and analyze the hypothesis of the Big Bang theory

## Course Requirements
- **Class Attendance and participation:** Each class will include in-class learning activities, making attendance necessary for this course. You are allowed up to three absences. If you face extenuating circumstances, please get in touch with me. The activities will vary from two-minute writing reflections, where you'll be asked to explain a concept in your own words and identify areas of confusion, to think-pair-share discussions with clicker questions. In the latter, you will answer multiple-choice questions that assess your understanding of lecture material, first independently and then in discussion with a partner, attempting to persuade each other of your reasoning. I will also provide two group worksheet exercises where you will actively analyze and interpret astronomical data. To measure your participation,  I will use a class response system. Participation points will be based on your effort, not the correctness of your answers. A total of 12 activities will be conducted throughout the semester, and completing at least 10 is required to earn full credit for participation. 

- **Reading before class:** The primary goal of a pre-reading assignment is to help students prepare for learning in the next class. You are expected to complete the assigned readings prior to class to ensure class time is used for engaging more deeply with the material. A portion of your grade will be based on completing reading questions before class. These questions are designed to assess your comprehension of the material, not simply test fact memorization, so careful attention to the readings is necessary. The questions must be submitted online at least 30 minutes before class, allowing me time to review your responses and adjust the lecture to address areas where more clarity is needed. 
- **Assignements:** The course is organized into six modules, and I will assign six assignments over the semester. You  are  allowed  to  submit  one  late  homework  assignment  at  no penalty. Additional late assignments will receive a maximum score of 50\% if submitted within one week after the due date. Assignments will receive a zero score if submitted more than one week after the due date. Each assignment will have two parts. The first part will include a series of short questions, similar to the in-class activities. You are encouraged to collaborate with your classmates on this portion but must submit your own individual copy, including the names of those you worked with. The second part will be an open-ended question completed in assigned groups. This part will require you to apply knowledge from the current unit (and previous ones) to a scenario you create. The assignments will be structured clearly and easily divided into distinct components, enabling students to progressively apply their knowledge through the scaffolding technique. Tentatively, here are six assignments:

    - The Universe is vast and it is hard to comprehend sizes, distances, and scales. This assignment will help build intuition by putting things in familiar terms by scaling their sizes. You will scale the Milky Way Galaxy and some famous objects within it to comprehend its vastness. You will also compare the age of the Universe to human lifetimes. Additionally, experimentally verify Kepler’s three laws of planetary motion and apply Newton’s laws of motion and gravitation to understand orbital dynamics. Submit a brief report including observations, calculations, and diagrams summarizing both parts.
    - With this assignment you will gain an understanding of the electromagnetic spectrum and the various types of telescopes. Learn when it is advantageous to place telescopes in space versus on Earth. Explore how light interacts with atoms and molecules and explain how the observed spectrum of light provides insights into the physical conditions of the emitting matter. Additionally, you will apply Doppler shift to infer the motion of a source.
    - In this assignment you will create your own Hertzsprung-Russell diagram using a provided list of stars and learn how stars are organized on this diagram based on their temperature and luminosity. This activity will help you explore how the HR diagram reflects the time evolution of stars and provides insights into a population of celestial objects. Additionally, you will compare your favorite star on the HR diagram with our Sun and explain your observations.
    - This assignment will give you insights into the formation of solar systems and the planets that orbit them. You will investigate the possibility of life on other planets and learn how exoplanets in other solar systems can be detected. Furthermore, you will investigate binary eclipsing systems and measure the mass and radii of the stars within these binary systems.
    - You will learn about different types of galaxies and how to classify them. Furthermore, you will explore evidence for the existence of dark matter by studying the rotation curves of stars within a galaxy.
    - After completing this assignment, you will understand how telescopes function as time machines when observing celestial objects. You will select a distant object beyond our Galaxy and determine its velocity and distance, then compare your findings with those of your group members, who will choose different distant objects. You will discuss the expansion of the Universe, explore how Hubble's Law relates to our understanding of the Universe's age, and examine the evidence supporting the Big Bang hypothesis.

## Academic Integrity

The University of Maryland is an academic community. Its fundamental purpose is the pursuit of knowledge. Like all other communities, the university can function properly only if its members adhere to clearly established goals and values. Essential to the fundamental purpose of the university is the commitment to the principles of truth and academic honesty. Accordingly, the Code of Academic Integrity is designed to ensure that the principle of academic honesty is upheld. The university's Code of Academic Integrity is a nationally recognized honor code, administered by a Student Honor Council. Any of the following acts, when committed by a student, shall constitute academic dishonesty:
- **Cheating:** fraud, deceit, or dishonesty in any academic course or exercise in an attempt to gain an unfair advantage and/or using or attempting to use unauthorized materials, information, or study aids in any academic course or exercise.
- **Fabrication:** unauthorized falsification or invention of any information or citation in any academic course or exercise.
- **Facilitating Academic Dishonesty:** knowingly helping or attempting to help another to violate any provision of this Code.
- **Plagiarism:** representing the words or ideas of another as one's own in any academic course or exercise.
- **Self-Plagiarism:** the reuse of substantial identical or nearly identical portions of one's own work in multiple courses without prior permission from the current instructor or from each of the instructors if the work is being submitted for multiple courses in the same semester.
- **Use of AI tools:** You are permitted to use generative technology, such as ChatGPT, for brainstorming, pre-writing, or researching topics relevant to your work. However, you must not include any AI-generated text in your submissions for this course. Please remember that you are responsible for verifying the accuracy of any AI-generated content before incorporating it into your assignment submissions.

If it is determined that an act of academic dishonesty has occurred, a grade of "XF" is the expected sanction for undergraduate students. The grade of "XF" is noted on the academic transcript as failure due to academic dishonesty. Lesser or more severe sanctions may be imposed when there are circumstances to warrant such consideration. Suspension or expulsion from the university may be imposed even for a first offense.
